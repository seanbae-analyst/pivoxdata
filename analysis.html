Step 1 — clean first

DataReady (defaults): readiness 73 → 78. Cleared 3,506 placeholder dates, removed 125 exact duplicate rows, converted 60 sentinel tokens to real empties — and refused, out loud, to impute GRADE or decide PII policy. The placeholders it cleared and the structure it exposed become findings 3 and 5 below.

Findings

1The file is not what it looks like

One row = one violation citation, not an inspection: 31,159 restaurants → 87,892 inspections → 295,685 rows (3.36 rows per inspection). Every naive COUNT(*) — "Manhattan had 109k inspections" — is violation-weighted and ~3.4× overstated.

2Averaging rows inflates the city's risk by 43%

SCORE repeats on every violation row of an inspection, so bad inspections get counted more: row-weighted mean 25.48 vs inspection-weighted 17.83 — a +42.9% bias (higher = worse). That's the difference between "the average inspection is a B" and "borderline C". A dashboard, a pasted-into-ChatGPT summary, or a fine-tuned model would all report the inflated number.

3The "missing grades" are mostly not a data problem

GRADE looks 50.7% broken — until you segment by inspection type: 62% blank on initial cycle inspections vs 4% on re-inspections (99.9% on administrative records, which never carry grades). The blanks are the process, not corruption. Imputing or dropping them would be the actual data-quality disaster — which is why the tool refuses imputation by design.

5The placeholder dates were hiding a backlog

All 3,506 cleared 1900-01-01 dates sit on rows with a blank inspection type: 3,506 establishments — 11.3% of all restaurants — recorded but never yet inspected (Manhattan 1,580 · Brooklyn 853 · Queens 698). A fake date wasn't just dirt; it was an un-inspected-restaurant queue stored where a date should be.

Recommendations

  • Reallocate, don't just add: shift initial-inspection capacity toward Queens and the five worst cuisines — per-inspection risk is highest there, while volume-based allocation over-serves Manhattan.
  • Burn down the never-inspected queue (3,506 establishments) as its own KPI — today it's invisible because it's encoded as a fake date instead of a status.
  • Publish a reporting contract with the data: entity keys, the grade-issuance rule, and "never average across violation rows." Findings 1–3 are silent 40–340% distortions waiting for every analyst and every AI prompt.

What happened to this analysis next

Each finding was generalized into a deterministic insight shape — granularity traps, weighted-average bias, structural missingness, concentration, segment gaps, outliers — that now runs on every file uploaded to DataReady after cleanup ("What your data says"). The engine re-discovers this page's headline findings on its own. Manual analysis as discovery; product as the generalization.

Limitations — snapshot analysis (no trend claims); avg score is a proxy, not adjudicated risk; cuisine labels are DOHMH's own; borough "0" rows (370) excluded from segment tables; gaps cited are decision-grade in magnitude but not confidence-intervaled.
